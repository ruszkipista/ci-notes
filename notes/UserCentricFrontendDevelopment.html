Introduction To User Experience Design

Often when people think about design, they think about the function that an object might have, whether it be a real world or a digital object. Now obviously function is important. If the object cannot carry out its desired function, then it has fundamentally failed. Yet the world is full of objects that do carry out their desired function but are frustrating to use, not pleasing to look at, or prone to improper use.

Form should follow function

Interacting with a thing should elicit a positive emotional response. Have you ever pulled a door handle that says push underneath? At some level you probably felt a bit stupid. We've all done it. But what if I told you it wasn't your fault? The door handle was invented to pull. If the door only pushes, why put a giant handle that just screams "pull me" above it? Maybe it should just have had a push plate on it. In fact, not 20 feet away from me right now is a door that does just that. It has a pull handle directly above a plate that says push. And everybody who walks through the door for the first time gets caught out by this. They pull when they should push.

And when you draw someone's attention to what is actually in place, there's often a sigh of relief on their part. They might have felt slightly embarrassed by not being able to open the door to a place of learning and experimentation. They experienced a negative emotional response to that interaction.

Terminology

User eXperience refers to the overall effect on a person when interacting with something. This can be positive, negative, neutral, and all points in between.

User eXperience Design (UXD) is the work, processes and skill sets involved in creating products that are useful, usable and provide value. It refers to the discipline of ensuring that a product not only works but is intuitive, simple, and enjoyable to use. It is what you do to make sure that the emotional experience is positive. Must come into play at the beginning of a product's life cycle, not tacked on near the end

USEFUL

to the user who wants to interact with your product.

USABLE

so that when users are interacting with your product, it's an enjoyable experience, iIt generates a positive emotional response.

VALUABLE

Not only valuable to the user but valuable to the organization who created the product in the first place.

User Centered Design is a defined process used to achieve a positive user experience. It is a process that you can follow to make sure users enjoy using your product again and again.

The user interface (UI) is the point at which human users interact with a computer, website or application. The goal of effective UI is to make the user's experience easy and intuitive, requiring minimum effort on the user's part to receive maximum desired outcome.

User Experience and User Interface on websites

User Experience (UX) is primarily the way the user feels when visiting your  website.  

User Interface (UI) is the way they use your site, the navigation, input fields and  buttons.

User Experience is about

  • Visually appealing content – colours, fonts, media etc.
  • Understanding the type of user coming to your page, and what their needs are.
  • Learnable – easy to understand, bite size information. Not overloading the user  with masses of text.
  • Clean simple design, using lots of space.
  • Subtle but effective reactions to user actions. For example hover effects and animations.  

User interface is about

  • Keeping to website conventions, so even a person brand new to your site can find things where they expect them to be.
  • Making things easy to find quickly.

Users only see what’s there

Most web users are impatient and are not likely to explore your entire site. They won’t spend ages looking for hidden functionality. If what they want is not easy to find, they will leave to find it somewhere else.  

  • Put what the user wants/needs in easy to find places
  • Keep it SIMPLE! You may be fascinated by all the details of your subject,  but the user probably just wants the bare essentials, so put those first.  And then link to more detail in a separate page if you think it is necessary.  

Users reply on familiar patterns

  • Users want intuitive websites, driven by their prior knowledge and association with other systems.  
  • They expect a navbar to be at the top of the page
  • They expect to be able to find contact details in the navbar
  • They expect to find social media links in the footer, (or in the navbar if there is space).  
  • They expect content to be laid out in easy to find ways.  
  • Be consistent in your use of colours, fonts and icons.
  • Look at other websites on the kind of topic you have chosen, notice the things  they have in common and try to keep to the same conventions in your own site.  

Users are not always in the mood to read

  • Attention spans are short, users will balk at large paragraphs of text and move on. Condense your information into the smallest most essential parts that you can, give them just what they need and no more.  
  • Use visuals (icons, graphs, images_ux/) where you can to display information in a way that is faster to understand.

Great examples of excellent UX / UI

  • Google - Clean, simple, does exactly what it should do and no more.  
  • Duolingo - consistent imagery, symbols etc, gives info in small bite size portions
  • Mailchimp - consistent, info in small bites, lots of space around the content.  
  • Simple - well-structured navigation, big headings that the user can read in a hurry  and locate the info that they are looking for easily.
  • Nest – great product photography, bite size information, lots of space, easy to find  what the user needs quickly.

Importance of user experience in web applications

For our purposes, we're going to focus on generating positive user experiences when interacting with web applications. So how will attention to user experience benefit your work as a web developer?

Well, every year thousands of applications and products are brought to market. That's a lot of competition. In order to rise above the noise and flourish, you need to take every advantage you can to attract and retain a healthy user base. And first impressions count. Public users have zero tolerance for poor experiences.

Also, poor experiences can reduce productivity amongst people who need to use a product as part of their daily work.

The question users ask when encountering a product is, "does this fit my needs"? But at a subliminal level, they're asking additional questions such as: "Do I like this place?", "Do I like being in here?", "Is it a place I want to return to?". These questions are often answered within seconds. And the goal of UX is to ensure the answer is "yes" every time.

In addition to a product's users, the product's owners are also looking for value. They ask questions like

  • What do I get in return for this investment?
  • Will I get positive reactions from my users?
  • Will this lead to more sales?
  • Will I get better conversions?
  • Can I create an established brand with added authority?
  • Can I increase traffic?

Another way of looking at UX in terms of web or software development is to look at it as a simple equation. UX can be seen as the sum of organizational goals, user goals, user interface, and interaction with a back-end system.

While UX isn't strictly a science, there is a process and a structure behind generating the conditions for a positive user experience. The methodology, or the process, was first developed by Jesse James Garrett, and they're known as the five planes of user experience. They are strategy, scope, structure, skeleton and surface, with strategy being the most abstract and surface being the most concrete. So strategy represents the business goals, the initial idea, why you want to build a product in the first place and the surface represents the finished product.

So let's look at the planes in more detail. First look at the strategy and move on up, bubble up, to the surface.

STRATEGY

What are we aiming to achieve in the first place and for whom?

A user experience strategy can be seen as a combination of Design Strategy and Business Strategy. It can be broken down into three main areas of interest.

Focus: what's worth doing?
Definition: what are we creating?
Value: what value does it provide?

SCOPE

Which features, based on information from the strategy plane, do you want to include in your design? (in other words, based on our business goals and our objectives and the idea and the reason behind the product's existence) what do we want to include into our design?

What's on the table for a production release and what's not, at least for now?

Remember, you can go live with what's called a Minimum Viable Product, an MVP. And a Minimum Viable Product will contain the essential features that allow the thing that you're building to function. You may have desirable features that you really want to put in there, but you may not have the time, the skill set, or the resources to do it at that particular stage.

STRUCTURE

How is the information structured and how are the features, elements, the data logically grouped?

Remember, a user is only visiting your site for some kind of content.

SKELETON

How will our information be represented, and how will the user navigate to the information and the features?

Things are getting more defined now. We're starting to create mock-ups, whether they're on paper or using tools to create prototypes. So it's concerned with how the information should be implemented.

How will the user navigate through the information and the features?

How will the content relate to each other? What relationships will the content have?

What has priority? What has top priority? What has lower priority? And based on those priorities, where do we position the content? How do we navigate to those higher and lower priorities?

SURFACE

What will the finished product look like?

What colors, typography, what images_ux/ and design elements, animations, transitions or other effects will we use?

Where does the UXD process sit?

Well, it's not just a step in the overall development of a product. It is the process that allows the product to get built in the first place. UX and in particular the five planes of UXD, otherwise known as user centered design, must be present in all stages of a product's evolution, from its inception to the first time a user uses or interacts with it in the real world.

So it's an ongoing effort to learn about users and evolve our products because a product is a living thing. There are too many competitors out there to rest on your laurels once you've built something. It's also not just limited to web design or development. And all project stakeholders contribute to user experience design.

Common activities of the 5 planes

Activities that are common to all the planes can be broken into tasks or functionality, and information. So from the most abstract to the concrete, you're focusing on tasks at any given time, and you're also working with information. For example, at the strategy level, the task may be to conduct user research, and the output of that may be the information that gives you enough to determine the scope at the next plane.

You further refine that into the functional specification. Also, we refine the information. The content becomes slightly more defined at that point.

And then moving on up, getting more concrete, we focus on interaction design. Interaction design is concerned with consistency of elements and consistency of relationships, the consistency of the relationships of the information discovered and uncovered in the informational design aspect.

It's also concerned with learnability, if something is learnable. Is it intuitive?

Intuitive means first-time learning. Can a user understand and comfortably navigate through a system upon seeing it for the first time?

Then moving up to the skeleton, we're concerned with interface design. That might be prototyping; developing paper-based prototypes or tool based prototypes.

We also start looking at the navigation design. Where will the placement of a navigation be? What is the order of the navigation, the hierarchy, the priorities? Where is it going to take us?

The information design becomes much more defined at this point. That's the arrangements of the elements that contain that information

And finally, the tasks at the surface level are what the user can do. How they can interact, what effects are there, what they can click on, where they can go to, and also the final version of the information that appears on the screen.

Planes are interdependent

Attempting to complete the work in one plane before moving on to the next will result in a poorer final outcome.

It forces you into a rigid process where work done on one plane is now written in stone and can't be returned to and revisited and revised.

So what you should do is allow the work on one plane to finish while work on the next is in progress. It will allow the planes to inform each other, and the result is a more fully realized product. So there is flexibility in this process.

Planes are interdependent, they're not worked on in isolation.

So the outputs of the structure plane form the inputs to the skeleton plane.

Choices made on each plane affects the choices available on the following planes.

Resume example

Strategy - focus - What’s Worth Doing?

You're a software developer looking for work opportunities. You're looking for projects, collaboration ideas, contract work, and working with startups. You need to get an online presence for you and for your work. You decide to build an online resume. You decide that that's what's worth doing.

Strategy - definition -What are we Creating?

Now you decide that Facebook won't cut it, it's too generic. We need to build a custom site. So that's what we decide to create.

Strategy - value - What Value does it Provide?

Well for you, the amount of work increases, you build a network of peers, and increase your client base. It's your calling card. And for the user, it's proof of ability. It gives people confidence in what you can do.

So in terms of these content requirements, how do they provide value? Well you've done some research.

  • Recruiters say they wanted a traditional CV format that's downloadable, possibly a PDF - so you can supply your content in that format.
  • Agencies want proof of ability - so the online resume itself stands as proof
  • and everybody wants to see your GitHub repositories.

Scope - Functional Specification

You take the resume idea, and decide what features will be available. So the features you might decide on would be

  • to allow people to request collaboration from you,
  • provide a traditional CV or resume download option for recruiters or more traditional employers

Scope - Content Requirements

In terms of content, you might decide on providing

  • personal details,
  • a personal pitch for collaborators,
  • your work history,
  • the projects that you've undertaken,
  •  a summary of your skill sets,
  • some groups, meetups, and conferences that you have attended or been a part of.

So they're the features.

Structure - Interaction Design

Interaction design is concerned with patterns and sequences that provide options to the user. The patterns might be the navigation, or the order of the menu items.

Structure - Information Architecture

We tend to read from left to right, so you might want to prioritize your top level information on the left with diminishing priority to the right.

You might decide on the relationships between the information:

  • personal information should go beside a personal pitch.
  • work history sits really well with the skills summary.
  • request for a collaboration goes on its own area.
  • the project listings go in their own area.
  • group all your organizations together with some kind of identifier, maybe a map.
  • the download is a link to an external file

The information architecture takes these relationships and attempts to prioritize them.

  • Our top priority might be displaying our personal information and our personal pitch first. That's what we assume that the user wants to see upon first arriving at our site.
  • My priority two might be displaying our work history and our skill summary.
  • Priority three might be displaying our project listings.
  • Priority four, then, becomes a request for a collaboration.
  • Priority five might be a list of the groups and the organizations that the developer is a part of.
  • Priority six is the ability to download a CV.

Next, having put the bulk of the tasks and assignments required at the structure level in place, we move on to the skeleton level.

Skeleton - Interface and Navigation Design

Interface design is concerned with the best arrangement and visual presentation of the elements.

Navigation design is concerned with intuitive navigation and the completion of tasks.

At this point, we also create so-called mock-ups. Mock-ups give us a good idea of how well constructed our information architecture is and also the arrangement of our features.

Here is a low fidelity mock-up made using Balsamiq:

So we have decided that our information architecture looks something like this:

  • We'll have personal info on the left, and we'll have the pitch on the right.
  • We'll place our navigation in the upper region, which is common and conventional.
  • And we'll have our profile image on the top left hand side.

So based on our information architecture, we prioritized our personal info and our pitch as being priority one. When a prospective employer arrives at your site for the first time, they'll immediately be able to determine your personal info and your personal pitch.

We arranged these also using, roughly, the law of thirds. The personal info takes up one third of the width of the screen, and the pitch takes up two thirds.

The same arrangement is put in place for the profile image and the name, the title, and the menu items associated with the developer.

We will  maintain this structure and arrangement throughout the site. The header and the footer are the same, and our main content areas, the shape of those areas, is the same. What's changed is the actual content. This reassures the user. They've already been exposed to this pattern on the home page.

Now, in the resume page, this pattern is repeated.

The Work History takes up one third, My Skills take up two thirds.

Now while the project submission, or project request form, is slightly different, it's still balanced:

We still have the header at the top, and we still have the footer at the bottom. The navigation hasn't changed.

Also in terms of navigation, the priority of our content is reflected in the navigation. So "Home" represents our personal info and our pitch that's on the left. And then we move to "Resume", to "Contact", to "Download CV".

Now you might be wondering, well there are only four menu items here. Where are the links to our GitHub repositories and the links to meetups, our organizations, and so on.

The reason they're not shown here is because in this particular module, we're using solely HTML, CSS and Bootstrap for the grid. The meetups and the GitHub repositories sections will require JavaScript, which we will learn in the next module.

Having carried out the tasks, the activities, the information gathering, and the organization across the strategy, the scope, the structure and the skeleton planes, we then move on to the final plane, which is the surface.

Surface

The surface is the most concrete plane. This is what the user gets to see, the final product. So it's concerned with

  • color, typography, effects and imagery.
  • It tries to reinforce the meaning of the content and guide the user through tasks and information in an intuitive way.
  • It's also concerned with minimizing cognitive overload and maximizing intuitive learning.
  • And it's also concerned with the appropriate content.

So in our example here, we have a dark band on the top and a dark band at the bottom. So our header and our footer have similar colors. And they will be repeated across the Homepage, the Resume, and the Contact. But not the Download CV because Download CV is merely a link to an external PDF file which can be viewed in the browser but outside our site itself.

The content itself, our main content across each page, will take the format of 1/3 and 2/3. You can also see that there's a balance between the image and the header information and the content below that. So 1/3, 2/3, 1/3, 2/3. Again, providing a visual balance.

We've also split our footer information into thirds as well. We have another "about me", another opportunity to download the CV, and the social icons which could link to Facebook, Pinterest, LinkedIn, and so on.

Strategy

Let's dig a little deeper into the strategy plane.

Some questions you might ask about your content, your features, and your product is, "is the content culturally appropriate?" That doesn't just apply to geographical or religious considerations, you might also want to take into consideration the culture of your audience:

  • Is it a corporate site?
  • Is it for the artistic community?
  • Is it for designers?
  • Is it for schools?
  • and so on.

So determine what's culturally appropriate for your audience.

Another question you might ask: "is the content relevant?"

So, for example, our fictitious developer, Rosie, has spent time as a sculptor and an embalmer. You might question the value or the relevance of having a blog on embalming or sculpture on a resume that's targeted towards software development recruiters.

Another question we might ask is "can we track and catalog content in an intuitive way?" "Does it match up?"

Our audience might be used to spreadsheets. Our audience might be used to virtual reality, augmented reality. How do we take care of the content?

And that leads into the next question: "is the technology appropriate?"

Do we want to use virtual reality as a way of presenting our resume? Probably not yet, but at some time in the future, maybe yes.

Business objectives

So the product developers either make improvements to an existing site or build a site with the intention of getting value back into the company. That value can be increased brand awareness, a monetary return on investment, and also social currency.

The users of the product are looking for value, and they're looking for perceived value. Any changes you make, any improvements you make, should count.

So these circular interactions feed into the life and the growth of a product.

You ask yourself, "why are we so special?" If you're building something, why are we so special? Who else is building this? How well are they doing it? Are they doing it in such a way that is better than we can ourselves because of the resources and skills we lack?

What are the tech considerations? Are we building for a mobile savvy demographic, are we building for a scientific community and so on.

The most important question is "why would a user want this?"

The case of "build it, and they will come" doesn't always work out like that. Do your research. Find out, ask questions. In order to do that research, here's a list of activities that you can undertake to make sure you're building something people need or want.

Do stakeholder interviews first. Interview people who are involved in making decisions to create the product in the first place. Find out what they want and what will give them value for having that product out in the wild being used.

Do a competitor review. Who else is doing this? What are the pros of what they're doing, and what are the cons? What are they missing? What are they doing, but they could be doing better?

Do user interviews and research. Ask people, would you use this? Ask them why. And also, what a user thinks they need isn't always exactly what they need. We get to that later on.

If you have an existing product out there, do an audit. Do a retrospective. Find out what people like about your product. Find out what they don't like about your product and why. And figure out what changes you can make.

Customer needs

When defining your business goals, one question you need to ask yourself is, who is my target audience? Is my product for businesses (B2B) or for general consumers (B2C)?

User interview / Research

Now when it comes to consumers, you may ask a different set of questions.

  • What makes a good [...] experience?
  • What makes a good video streaming experience?
  • What makes a good flight booking experience?
  • What makes a good ecommerce experience?
  • When you go to a particular site, where do you go first? Why?
  • What would you put off doing when you got there and why?
  • How often do you visit the application? How often do you use it?
  • What do you most use it for?
  • What do you do before or after using the product?

If it's a business application you're building, often what you're attempting to do is to automate a series of manual tasks that a business user undertakes as part of their day-to-day functions. So to get a better idea of what they need, do your research. Interview potential users of your system, ask questions such as

  • What is your day-to-day role?
  • What functions do you perform?
  • What other functions are present?
  • How do these functions interact?
  • How does it compare with other experiences?
  • What technology and systems do you use, and what other systems are also used as part of your job?

Customer Behaviors

When it comes to purchasing something online for B2C customers

  • it's a simple emotional transaction
  • They don't generally visit the site that often, and it can be an impulse buy.
  • Often, there isn't much process behind the purchase.

When it comes to B2B purchases, much more thought goes into the purchase. For example, a need may be recognized, something that isn't available in the company at the moment.

  • We then determine the product's specifics.
  • We do some research.
  • We select a product and buy that product.
  • We also evaluate the product over time to determine whether you'll continue using that product.

Psychological Factors

The emotional triggers for B2C are the feelings that the product or service gives the consumer which leads them to complete a purchase.

Branding is a huge factor.

Emotional triggers behind B2B purchases are

  • risk aversion,
  • fear of getting it wrong, using the company budget to buy something that potentially may not work in the long run, having committed to it.

Pricing Models

For B2C, the pricing is generally transparent.

All users are charged the same price, unless there's discount codes available,

Shipping fees are often expected.

Up-selling and cross-selling is common.

But with B2B, it's a lot more complex.

The pricing can differ depending on the needs of the client.

And often the sites don't list all of the costs associated with the product or the services.

That's usually ascertained via follow-on phone call.

They have distinct plans, and there are often additional fees for implementation and maintenance charges.

Buyer Decision Making

In the B2C space, you're often only interacting with one person and in general, that person doesn't need to justify their purchasing decision.

And there's often very little forward planning.

The internet thrives on impulse buying.

But B2B is different. There's almost always a team of people behind the purchaser.

There's a set of procedures to be followed.

There's a complex decision-making process.

Research can often begin months before a purchase takes place.

Product Considerations

You've got to make sure that the content strategy is up to par.

It has to be catchy and compelling.

Catchy and compelling headlines are needed.

Don't overload the website with content.

Minimal content and large images_ux/ will work. As you can see there on the left hand side, there's an Apple site. Very little content, very strong emotive imagery and just the logo.

The design must be unique and stand out amongst the competition because the B2C space is crowded with competitors.

The journey between a user clicking "buy now" and actually making a payment must have a minimal amount of steps. In general, there's a three step rule of thumb that applies.

And most users will make their purchase on one of their first few visits.

Purchases almost never happen on the first visit.

  • Lead nurturing is important.
  • The content should include compatibility and integration information to assure the prospective customer.

Users on the B2B side are looking for as much information as possible.

As we mentioned, unique design is less important.

There should be lots of call to action (CTA) points: live chat, phone, text, email, everything to create a sense of reliability and assurance.

You should also supply various types of content.

Video tutorials, white papers to show that you're a thought leader and authority in this area, blogs, testimonials, frequently asked questions, and webinars.

Content is King

And the other thing to keep in mind whenever you're building something is that content is king. It's the reason why people use an app or website in the first place. So for example, here's a Venn diagram:

On the left, we have things on the front page of a university website. So you have campus photo slideshow, alumni in the news, promotions for campus events, press releases, letter from the president, statement of the school's philosophy, and virtual tour. I've seen these on many sites.

But, in general, what people are looking for are practical stuff, things like the list of faculty phone numbers and emails, the campus address, application forms, the campus police phone number, academic calendar, departments, course lists, parking information, and a usable campus map.

So just because you have content, it doesn't mean it's the right content. Make sure you have the right content.

What's worth doing?

We've got to figure out what's worth doing in the first place. User experience design is driven by business goals and user needs.

Figuring out what’s worth doing, remember, the users aren't you. You've got to find out what the user needs, not what you think the user needs in the first place. So we've got to figure out who's our target audience, our demographics? What experiences are compelling to them? And how is our offering, or proposed offering, different from our competitors and substitutes?

So then you ask, what does the user expect?

The best (intuitive) UX creates single-use learning. So when a user first lands will ask either consciously or unconsciously:

  • What is this? Is this what I expected to see?
  • Does it look credible and trustworthy?
  • Does it offer what I want?
  • Does it look valuable enough for me to stay?
  • Does it look valuable enough for me to return?
  • What actions can I take now?
  • How do I learn more?
  • How do I contact someone?

All of these must be addressed, and the answers must be available almost immediately.

Make sure you document the customer needs and the business objectives. It doesn't have to be a 45 page document, but write something down. Otherwise, you'll end up with the project below:

It's an old, frequently referenced visual representation of how information can be miscommunicated verbally.

So you have what the marketing requested, how the project team understood it, how did how the designer designed it, how the developer built it, and then what the customer really needed. So bear this in mind: written communication is essential.

We should create a roadmap that provides value for the users and the business

So one approach that you can use is to create a two-dimensional diagram. On the y-axis, we have importance, on the x-axis we have viability and feasibility. Importance, how crucial it is that we solve this problem for the business; and viability / feasibility, how realistic it is that we can implement a solution, do we have the skills, the resources, or the time to do this, to carry out or to implement requested features.

So as we go further up the y-axis, these features are deemed to be more and more important. And as we move out along the x-axis, the features are deemed to be more feasible or viable.

Map the requested features out on a spreadsheet and break them down into the opportunities and the problems to be solved.

So in our case, one of our features is to create an online presence.

A second might be to increase GitHub followers.

A third might be to become a blogger to increase your influence, to become a thought leader.

Another might be to showcase your work portfolio.

And a last one listed here could be to increase recruiter interaction.

Now rate them with two numbers (on scale of 1-5): one for Importance, one for Viability / Feasibility.

On a scale of 1 to 5, we've decided the most important thing is to create an online presence.

We've then decided, well, is it in our interest right now to increase GitHub followers? If we're building this for ourselves, for example if the developer is you, then at this point in your development career, you may not have many repositories. So the opportunity to increase GitHub followers can take place further down the line, so, for now, it's of low importance, but over time it will become more important.

Scope

We've seen that one of the activities undertaken at the strategy plane is determining what's on the table and what's off the table. What trade-offs are necessary in terms of business objectives and user needs? We refine that further at the scope level by determining what features of those business objectives and user needs are on and off the table.

When determining what's on the table, a defined scope uncovers and resolves conflicts early. Try and get a good picture of the features and the content that's available at this point. Document what's in and what's out for now. It prevents misunderstandings later.

If your scope is too big, you may be faced with the possibility of unrealistic deadlines, which will result in slippage, not meeting your milestones: your release dates, your commitment dates.

You can also suffer from scope creep. Scope creep is the addition of features that weren't agreed on at the starting point. Scope creep is notorious in software development. Be aware of it, recognize it, and always try to minimize it.

New ideas for features can come up any time, but they can be postponed for future releases. So identify what needs to be done now and what can wait.

On the other hand, if your scope is too small, you may build a product that's pretty unimpressive. It may be just a single page website with a list of your achievements and a paragraph of text.

Trade-offs

Here's a quote to bear in mind: trade-offs are essential to strategy: they create the need for choice, and they purposefully limit what a company offers.

Let's take IKEA’s strategic trade-offs as an example.

Now while they have limited customer services as you can wander for 30 minutes through some of IKEA's departments and not meet a member of staff, they do have intuitive shopping and lower prices to compensate. There's a very defined mapping as to how you move through the areas.

Another trade-off is it's got a flat pack and knock-down furniture. But the positive side of that is it's got in-house modern style and design.

IKEA is consistently located in suburban locations. Well, the money they save by having such vast warehouse style department stores is that they can stock an expansive on-site inventory. The store is the warehouse.

Another positive take on a trade-off is the long WOW!.

So pack in some minimal viable product features up front, and unfold new experiences over time. You can evolve and integrate. Consistently surprise and delight your users with small incremental releases that have perceived value.

From Why to What

We can separate requirements roughly into three sets:

  • what users and stakeholders say they need,
  • what they actually need, and
  • what they don't know they need.

Here's an example of making false assumptions about what users need:

In the image we can see there are pathways designed, possibly, by a town planner. And on paper, that probably looked fantastic. And in the 3D model, it looked equally impressive. But users of this system quickly learned that if they shortcut, they can get to the other path more quickly.

So the actual user experience differed from the design and the expected user experience. So we make confident but false assumptions about future behavior. There's a big difference between imagining how something will be used and how it'll actually be used. And often our preferences are governed by our emotions and our environment.

So how do we go about determining what people actually need?

  • identify what is requested, but not really needed,
  • research what is actually needed, but not requested.

Well, it can be easy to imagine a solution for a requirement, but solutions can often only address a symptom, rather than the underlying problem.

Explore the requirement to uncover the real problem, and focus on the problem, not the solution.

We might decide that as part of our resume, we're going to build gamification inside, that we're going to have chat apps and so on. But we're focusing on the solution. The problem really is: how do I get more work opportunities?

Here's a good quote. "People don't want to buy a quarter-inch drill, they want a quarter-inch hole." Good words of wisdom from Theodore Levitt.

What people really need, you'll often only find that out through brainstorming, exploring alternatives, additional research, and discussion. Discuss, talk, hammer out ideas, explore all avenues. Use mind mapping as part of your brainstorming techniques. It will save a lot of time and effort later in preventing you from building something that people don't need.

Requirement Types

Remember, requirements are needs, not features.

The focus isn't "we need a website". The focus should be "how can employers easily evaluate my skills? A website can service that need.

The success of any solution for a need is the product of two aspects:

  • what it does (functionality, features)
  • how well it performs against defined parameters (non-functional attributes, acceptance criteria, service levels)

Scope is concerned with requirements of five types

Objective

What does the user want to accomplish?

Business Rules

What dynamic constraints do we have to meet? (Calculations, definitions, legal, etc.) All software tries to model the real world and the rules that apply in that. And often, a product is a virtual representation of the business. So defining and refining the rules of how a business operates is extremely important if you want to map that to a piece of software.

Functional Requirements (FRs)

Functional requirements express function or feature and define what is required, e.g.

  • Visit customer site
  • Obtain conference venue

The requirements do not state how a solution will be physically achieved.

  • Drive to customer site is one possible solution. However, fly to customer site or travel by train to customer site are potential alternative solutions which may be worth consideration
  • Build conference centre is one possible solution. Hire a hotel room is an alternative solution Stating requirements early in the project as what rather than how allows room for flexibility and innovation later.

Content Requirements

As well as functional requirements, the scope plane is concerned with content requirements.

Mixed content works together to fulfill a requirement. It can be a combination of video, images_ux/, text, audio, and games. Take into consideration the format, the purpose, the size of the videos, the size of the images_ux/ and so on because they'll affect what are called non-functional requirements.

Contextual Content is governed by:

  • Psychological state
  • Learning abilities
  • Wants and needs

Non-functional Requirements (NFRs)

Non-functional Requirements define how well, or to what level a solution needs to behave. They describe solution attributes such as security, reliability, maintainability, availability (and many other “...ilities”), performance and response time, e.g.

  • responding within 2 seconds
  • being available 24 hours per day, every day

These NFRs may be:

  • Solution-wide or impacting a group of functional requirements: e.g.
  • All customer facing functionality must carry the company logo
  • All customer-facing functionality must respond within 2 seconds to requests
  • Related to a particular functional requirement, e.g.
  • Hire conference venue might have NFRs of accessibility, security, and availability

Requirement Types

We discussed how requirements can be broken into categories. And within those categories, they can be further broken down into types. And at a high level, that's useful, buildable and sellable.

  • So is what we're building useful to a customer? Is it useful to us?
  • Also, can we build it in the first place? Can we build it affordably in terms of time and resources?
  • Is it sellable? Could we sell this thing if we had it? If we build this thing, could we sell it?

Agile approach

An agile approach means committing to small, bite-sized increments of development. That way, if you find yourself going off track, you can recover within a short space of time because you're committing to solving a smaller part of the larger problem, rather than committing to the entire problem at one time.

It also is committed to minimal documentation. And in doing so, it encourages you to keep things simple. So keep the features simple, keep the schedule controllable, and set out your key milestones.

All features should align with the strategy. So the trade-offs determined in that two-dimensional graph should always be kept in mind when determining the features that must align themselves to the user needs and the business goals.

A component of the agile methodology is called a sprint. A sprint is a duration in time in which you commit to building something. A sprint can be a day or two, it can be a week, it can be a couple of weeks. In general, they're short, rarely goes over a month. In most cases, a sprint involves  1 or 2 weeks of development and collaboration.

You might determine that for the first sprint, you identify the CV elements, so just the elements that would go on a CV. And once you have those elements in place, you will use those to feed into the solution for sprint 2, which might be identifying online CV elements.

User Story

A user story is a tool used in Agile software development to capture a description of a software feature from an end-user perspective. A user story describes the type of user, what they want and why. A user story helps to create a simplified description of a requirement.

User stories are written in everyday language and describe a specific goal (what) from the perspective of an individual (who) along with the reason (why) he/she wants it.

In software development, the goal is often a new product feature, the individual is some type of end-user and the reason is the benefit that the user sees in the targeted product feature.

So in our case, our users are recruiters, employers, and collaborators. And the stories are the interactions with the system. They can be obtaining contact information, obtaining work history, obtaining a skill set, contacting the developer. So we need to flesh out how those stories can be made reality.

Here's an example of some more detailed scenarios.

A collaborator chooses to work with a developer.

In order to make that decision, the requirements might be the ability to view his skill sets, the ability to view contact details, the ability to submit a project outline for collaboration.

Another scenario is a recruiter chooses to work with a developer. How do they arrive at that?

Well, in order to do that, they may also want the ability to view skill sets, the ability to view contact details, and the ability to see work history.

User Story Template

User stories only capture the essential elements of a requirement:

  • Who it is for?
  • What it expects from the system?
  • Why it is important (optional?)?

Here is a simple format of user story used by 70% of practitioners:

Role - The user should be an actual human who interacts with the system.

  • Be as specific as possible
  • The development team is NOT a user

Action - The behavior of the system should be written as an action.

  • Usually unique for each User Story
  • The "system" is implied and does not get written in the story
  • Active voice, not passive voice ("I can be notified")

Benefits - The benefit should be a real-world result that is non-functional or external to the system.

  • Many stories may share the same benefit statement.
  • The benefit may be for other users or customers, not just for the user in the story.

Story Estimation

A story point is a relative metric used in agile project management and development to estimate the difficulty of implementing a given user story, which is an abstract measure of effort required to implement it. In simple terms, a story point is a number that tells the team about the difficulty level of the story. Difficulty could be related to complexities, risks, and efforts involved.

When the development team conducts an estimation, it is recommended to abandon the traditional “human-day” assessment method, and use Fibonacci numbers (1, 2, 3, 5, 8, 13, 21…) to estimate the story point.

While estimating story points, we assign a point value to each story. Relative values are more important than the raw values. A story that is assigned 2 story points should be twice as difficult as a story that is assigned 1 story point. It should also be two-thirds of a story that is estimated 3 story points.

In addition, it is important to note that when the single story point of the assessment is greater than 21, the user story needs to be split again, and the single user story point is no more than 8 is the most rational state.

Story point estimation is typically performed at the Product Backlog Grooming Sessions and the Product Backlog is evaluated by the team who is responsible for the actual development and testing work.

The estimated user stories make the Sprint Planning more efficient in practice. Sprint Planning determines which user stories will be implemented in the next Sprint. As each sprint runs the same length, the given team completes roughly around the same total number of story points (velocity) in each sprint. So the Sprint Planning aims to come up with a list of stories to be implemented, totalling story points up to the team’s velocity.

Structure

The structure plane is concerned with the organization of your functionality and your content and how to navigate an intuitive way through your content and your features.

   

Some questions you might ask when engaging in activities at the structure plane are

  • How to get to a particular location?
  • Where do you go once you get there?
  • categories of information

Structure also addresses the way information is presented, how the information is organized. And it strives to ensure that the organization and presentation is intuitive.

Structure should be appropriate for the intended audience.

In this example, you can see on the y-axis, the structure moves from simple to complex. On the x-axis, it moves from linear to nonlinear.

So what are the trade-offs here? Well, a linear narrative is easier to discern, and you can learn it pretty quickly. Nonlinear can be quite complex. But there are always pros and cons. A linear narrative can be quite restrictive. A nonlinear hyperlink narrative can be quite flexible but possibly confusing.

So choose the structure for your audience, for your culture.

Interaction Design (IXD)

Interaction design is also a concern of the structure plane.

  • Interaction design is responsible for defining the structure and the behavior of interactive systems.
  • It creates meaningful relationships within the elements, the content, and the information.
  • And it communicates functionality and interactivity.

  • It also reveals workflows.
  • It reduces the risk of user error.
  • And it makes users aware of state changes on the system.

The interaction design can be broken into five particular concerns.

So ensuring that your site is consistent, predictable, learnable, visible, and it provides clear and intuitive feedback.

Consistent, Predictable

Years ago, when web development and web design was more or less the Wild West, navigation elements could and did go anywhere on a site. Often, developers relied on the browser navigation buttons to carry out navigation for them. Nowadays, the development community has settled on conventions. And as a result, users develop an expectation of those conventions.

By the way, never rely on the browser forward and back buttons to act as a substitute for your application's navigation.

For example, now, navigation elements appear in a horizontal fashion close to the top of your browser window.

All your elements and interactive features should be consistent, both in their coloring, their theming, and so on.

So consistency is the key.

When a scenario is acted out, any transitions, rollovers, and tooltips that are used as part of the scenario should behave consistently. Leverage the visitor's prior experience, both here on your site and elsewhere.

There should be a consistency of voice. Labels and terms should be the same throughout the experience. Content and imagery should have a stable, consistent style.

Only be different when it makes something better. That applies to the navigation that we saw a moment ago and also the elements, or your content layout. People have expectations.

Visible

All your elements should be discoverable. There should be no luck involved. And you should include things like content hinting.

Content hinting occurs when you partially reveal elements of your page just above the fold. The fold is the bottom of your browser window. Because if you don't do that, the user won't know what's below, and they won't be encouraged to scroll. So make sure content is visible wherever possible.

Learnable

Again, back to the phrase single-trial learning. It should be intuitive. Users have come to expect that on hovering their mouse, or rolling over their cursor over navigation links, that if there's sub-navigation elements, they'll be revealed through sub-menus.

Stick to that convention unless you're actively trying to improve this, or you're deliberately experimenting. But if the focus is to sell clothing on an e-commerce site, all of these elements should be more or less visible to the user based on their prior experience. Don't distract from the primary purpose of your application.

Predictable

If you can answer yes to the following questions: "Where am I?, "How did I get here?", "What can I do here?", "Where can I go from here?", then you've provided a strong sense of place, you've set the correct expectations, and you've made it possible for people to accurately predict the outcome of their interactions.

Feedback

Instructional design also provides feedback. So for example, we have a 5 day coding challenge. And we encourage you to sign up for the 5 day code coding challenge and submit your details.

Now we mentioned earlier that interaction design provides feedback in the form of a clearly visible change in state.

So if you look on the left-hand side, we have our 5 day coding challenge. The form contains a button that has yet to be clicked. It hasn't been submitted yet, so the text is in white, and the background is in orange.

Once the user has submitted their information, the system should provide feedback to let them know that something is happening. In this case, the system provides feedback in the form of a color change and a text change in the bottom. So the text on the button went from "Sign Up Now" to "Processing".

Clear feedback to the user makes them feel assured.

Instructional design should also provide feedback in the case of user error.

If for some reason there's a broken link on your site, or a user may have typed in an incorrect URL, they'll be taken to a "page not found" area, which is a 404 error.

So provide clear information to the user telling them that an error has occurred and also provide a link that will take them back to safety.

Information Architecture

Another main concern of the structure plane is information architecture. It is responsible for the creation of organization and navigational schemas and in doing so, it allows users to efficiently move through content and be easily educated and informed as they do so.

Good information architecture also allows owners to accommodate growth and change. So as your application or your site evolves and more content and more structure is added, the architecture can facilitate that change.

Let's have a look at the information architecture types.

Tree

So the most common, standard one is the tree structure. It reduces complexity by revealing information and structure as you navigate and traverse down and up through the tree.

But it can be problematic on mobile devices. Although that has been resolved somewhat by the convention of using the Burger icon, where the Burger icon is a representation of your navigation elements collapsed into a single icon. So when you click on the icon, these elements can be revealed layer by layer. If a user needs to go beyond three clicks to get to their destination, the chances are that you're introducing cognitive overload.

Nested List

Another type is the nested list, which is well-suited to mobile devices. it's a linear path to detail. It reduces complexity, but because it's a very defined path to your location, it can prevent exploring.

Hub and Spoke

Another popular one on mobile devices is the hub-and-spoke. It has a central home with links to spokes containing subsections of information.

It works well for multi-featured apps. But you can't navigate between the spokes themselves. You must come back to the home to move to another spoke.

Dashboard

A dashboard is very useful for at-a-glance, key information. The elements on the primary dashboard can be expanded to show detailed views of that information. So the dashboard view shows summary information, and the elements of the dashboard can be clicked to reveal detailed information. Dashboards, in general, are more suited to desktop applications.

Principles of Organization

So, for example, we have a corporate website. Now, the organizing principle behind the navigation elements here is the audience.

So we have a consumer audience, we have a business audience, and we have an investor audience. Let those form the top-level elements of our information architecture.

On a travel website, geography can be the organizing principle: You have North America, Europe, Africa, and so on.

Rules of Thumb of Organising

  • The organizing principles used at the highest levels of the site should be the most closely tied to user needs and business objectives.

  • Those at lower levels are usually influenced by feature specifications and content requirements.
  • Now every collection of information has a built in conceptual structure, and in most cases, it has more than one.
  • The hard part is creating the right structure for your objectives and the user's needs.

Concerned with

  • How will the users get around?
  • How do we present the content?

We want to introduce specific content at the most relevant and most appropriate points in the experience. And we add immediate value with each tap or click. And primarily, we want to add positivity to the overall experience over time with each interaction.

Navigation Design

Navigation is concerned with how you find what you want. So how do you navigate?

  • browsing via navigation systems, such as links and menu items.
  • searching via keywords and phrases.
  • using filters to narrow large lists and sets of information.
  • use pagination controls to provide information architecture and a sense of depth and breadth.

An example of pagination controls would be an airline booking system. The airline could easily present all of the information that they require from you in order to book a flight on one form. But that is guaranteed to induce cognitive overload on behalf of the user. So that's why we use pagination to move from next page to next page.

We also have progressive disclosure in the form of progress bars, indicating, possibly, that you're at step 1 of 5, you're at step 2 of 5, and so on and providing information of what's happening at that step.

Cognitive Friction

Cognitive friction occurs when a user is confronted with an interface or affordance that appears to be intuitive but delivers unexpected results

You should  reduce cognitive friction by ensuring that navigation elements are

  • visible and easily findable
  • don't require time to decode

To provide consistency, we ensure the same interactions and behaviors appear across our content.

You can see that somebody went to a lot of effort to present this information. But too much information is worse than no information at all.

All your hard-earned work should be almost taken for granted. Because bad design will annoy a user. The following are examples of poor informational design.

  • a form that's difficult to complete, resulting in errors
  • Poor instructions that cause frustration or even danger.
  • Poor educational materials that don't promote learning.
  • Poorly represented scientific data that's misinterpreted.
  • Manufacturing UIs that don't warn of potential danger.
  • Websites that are difficult to navigate and unpleasant to look at.
  • Information that isn't relevant to the reason we're here.

Context and order

Context and order matter. As users of online entities, we have developed a convention. So for example, you may be representing information of somebody such as state, email address, personal details. But we expect to see the name first followed by, possibly, the title, company, street address, city, and so on.

There's a hierarchy of priority there, or a hierarchy of importance. Follow that.

Use alphabetical ordering when information is referential, such as contact lists and dictionaries.

We then use categorical ordering when information is similar and clustered.

So we have a category, a high level category, of courses, partners, more info, blogs, and so on. And the courses will expand to show different course titles. So they are clustered, and they're similar. And there's a category of information there, so move them accordingly. You're not going to put course details in the blog section.

We then use location ordering when wayfinding and orientation is needed. A sports application, for example, or a geography site. We can display our information using location ordering.

With things like timelines, or, in our case, in our resume, our work history, we use time ordering to compare fixed duration events. A fixed duration is an amount of time that our developer would have worked at a particular location.

For our form, keep it simple. Remember the Hick-Hyman law.

Surface

What appears on the surface plane?

You develop a visual language that provides information and context, color, layout, fonts, images_ux/, order and sequence, and the site's identity.

A visual language also provides economy. The most important elements are easily recognized. In our example here, the navigation items pop out and instantly show what kind of information is available to the user. The content is split into two areas: "personal info" and "about me". It's minimized. There is progressive disclosure. As you move through the navigation elements, content will be progressively disclosed to the user.

Readability

  • Your content should be legible.
  • There should be a font contrast, and
  • there should be consistent patterns.

Fonts/Typography

  • should be visually distinct,
  • indicate different information
  • should be consistent

We can see the pattern here on the Resume page is similar to the pattern on the Home page.

  • There's a contrast between the background color in the work history and my skills.
  • There's a color contrast between the skill percentages and the backgrounds.
  • There's a contrast between the font. For example the section titles use a font called XO, whereas the main content uses a font called Roboto.
  • The patterns are also consistent. You can see that the header section and the footer section are consistent across the pages, as is the shape of the main content areas.
